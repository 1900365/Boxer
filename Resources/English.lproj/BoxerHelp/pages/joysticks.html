Playing with your joystick

You can play joystick-supporting DOS games in Boxer with a real joystick, gamepad or racing wheel—or even with your iPhone. There are two ways to get started:

A: Connect a USB or Bluetooth game controller

Boxer supports any USB or Bluetooth game controller recognised by OS X. Most controllers will work out-of-the-box: just connect the device to your Mac and then launch your game in Boxer. Boxer will activate joystick emulation and your game should find the emulated joystick automatically.

Some controllers require additional drivers before OS X will recognize them, and others may not be compatible with OS X at all. If Boxer and other OS X apps don’t see your controller, consult the device manufacturer for support.

To get OS X to recognise an XBOX 360 controller, you need to install third-party drivers. Get the drivers here

To get OS X to recognise a Playstation 3 SIXAXIS controller, you need to pair the device with your Mac. Watch a tutorial on pairing the controller

…or B: Connect an iPhone or iPod Touch running Joypad

Joypad is an application for iOS that makes your iPhone or iPod Touch into a game controller for your Mac.

You can get Joypad from the iOS App Store. Once you have it, start up Joypad on your iOS device and then launch your game in Boxer. After a short delay, Boxer should connect to Joypad and you can begin controlling your game with it.

If Boxer does not connect to Joypad initially, try closing and relaunching Joypad on the iOS device. Consult Joypad’s Support FAQ for more tips.

Joystick emulation options

By default, Boxer emulates a simple DOS-era 4-button joystick. It can also emulate DOS flightsticks or racing wheels instead, to better suit the game you’re playing and the capabilities of your own controller.

While playing, you can press Cmd ⌘+I to bring up the Joystick Inspector and choose a different kind of joystick to emulate. The choices are:

Standard joystick/gamepad (default)

In this mode, Boxer emulates a simple DOS joystick or gamepad with 4 buttons and up to 4 axes (depending on how many axes your own game controller has.)

This is the most reliable option and suitable for most DOS games. Note that older DOS games may only support the first 2 buttons and axes.

On a modern gamepad: the left thumbstick is mapped to the “X” and “Y” axes in DOS, and the right thumbstick to the “X2” and “Y2” axes. The directional pad also acts as “X” and “Y”.

Thrustmaster Flight Control System (FCS)

The granddaddy of flightsticks, widely supported by DOS flight simulators: offers 4 buttons, a 4-way hat switch, and rudder pedals (if you have enough axes on your real controller.)

On a modern gamepad: the left thumbstick is mapped to the “X” and “Y” axes, while the horizontal axis of the right thumbstick acts as the rudder. The directional pad is used as the hat switch.

CH FlightStick Pro

Another popular and widely-supported DOS flightstick: this has 4 buttons, a 4-way hat switch, rudder pedals and a throttle wheel.

Owing to a design limitation, the FlightStick Pro only recognises one button-press at a time. If you don’t need the extra throttle axis, use Thrustmaster instead.

On a modern gamepad: the left thumbstick is mapped to the “X” and “Y” axes. The horizontal axis of the right thumbstick acts as the rudder, and the vertical axis as the throttle. The directional pad is used as the hat switch.

Racing Wheel

A generic DOS-era racing wheel: with 4 buttons and brake/accelerator pedals.

The brake and accelerator inputs are combined on the “Y” axis, but also sent separately on the “X2” (brake) and “Y2” (accelerator) axes. If you’re playing a racing game that can use pedals on separate axes, tell it to use those for more precise control.

On a modern gamepad: the left thumbstick is used for steering, while the right thumbstick is accelerator/brake. If you have an XBOX 360 controller, the triggers act as brake and accelerator pedals.

If you are using Joypad, you can steer in this mode by tilting your iPhone/iPod Touch left and right. This will make you look really cool.
